# Common Deck Types

Use this reference to adapt the workflow to common presentation categories.

This list is intentionally broader than academic talks. For each type, adjust the guide, generator, and review priorities instead of forcing one generic template onto every deck.

## 1. Project Status Or QBR

Goal:

- Help stakeholders understand progress, risks, decisions, and next steps quickly.

Style:

- Clean, direct, data-forward.
- Strong summary slides and decision flags.

Must-have sections:

- Status summary
- Progress against goals
- Risks and blockers
- Decisions needed
- Next-period plan

Common pitfall:

- Turning the deck into a raw activity log instead of an executive update.

## 2. Decision Memo Or Proposal Deck

Goal:

- Help the audience choose a path or approve a proposal.

Style:

- Argument-driven, concise, evidence-backed.
- Each slide should move the audience toward a decision.

Must-have sections:

- Context
- Problem
- Options
- Recommendation
- Cost, risk, and tradeoffs

Common pitfall:

- Describing everything you know instead of making the decision obvious.

## 3. Executive Or Board Review

Goal:

- Give senior stakeholders a high-signal view of health, trajectory, risk, and decisions.

Style:

- Sparse, high-trust, low-drama.
- Big numbers and implications matter more than process detail.

Must-have sections:

- Top takeaways
- Critical metrics
- Material risks
- Strategic asks or decisions

Common pitfall:

- Letting operational detail bury the board-level message.

## 4. Sales Pitch Or Customer Proposal

Goal:

- Convince a prospect that your solution is worth buying or piloting.

Style:

- Outcome-oriented and customer-specific.
- Proof, case studies, and ROI matter more than feature dumping.

Must-have sections:

- Customer problem
- Proposed solution
- Evidence or case study
- ROI or impact
- Commercial path and next steps

Common pitfall:

- Leading with company history or feature lists instead of the buyer's problem.

## 5. Investor Pitch

Goal:

- Earn trust and show why the company can grow into an attractive investment.

Style:

- Confidence with restraint.
- Clean narrative arc and financial credibility.

Must-have sections:

- Problem
- Product or solution
- Market
- Traction
- Business model
- Team
- Financial outlook

Common pitfall:

- Overselling visuals while underspecifying traction, market logic, or economics.

## 6. Training, Onboarding, Or Workshop Deck

Goal:

- Teach the audience how to do something or think about a process.

Style:

- Guided, scaffolded, example-heavy.
- Repetition and signposting help more than flashy design.

Must-have sections:

- Objectives
- Concepts or process steps
- Worked examples
- Practice or exercises
- Recap

Common pitfall:

- Dense reference slides that work as documentation but fail as teaching material.

## 7. Paper Reading Or Research Talk

Goal:

- Help the audience understand the problem, method, evidence, and limitations.

Style:

- Claim-evidence-explanation.
- Figures and tables are evidence, not filler.

Must-have sections:

- Cover
- Context and problem
- Method overview
- Key evidence
- Limits or discussion
- Takeaways
- Closing

Common pitfall:

- Replaying the paper section by section instead of building a presentation storyline.

## 8. Webinar, Keynote, Or Product Launch

Goal:

- Hold attention while introducing a theme, product, or narrative for a broad audience.

Style:

- More theatrical and visually unified than an internal report.
- Strong pacing and clean reveals.

Must-have sections:

- Big opening
- Framing problem or trend
- Main story beats
- Product or message reveal
- Takeaways and close

Common pitfall:

- Mixing keynote theatrics with cramped, detail-heavy slides.

## 9. Incident Review Or Postmortem

Goal:

- Explain what happened, why it happened, what changed, and how risk is reduced.

Style:

- Calm, factual, timeline-friendly.
- Clarity and accountability matter more than brand polish.

Must-have sections:

- Incident summary
- Timeline
- Root causes
- Impact
- Remediations
- Follow-up owners

Common pitfall:

- Turning the review into blame assignment or technical noise instead of actionable learning.

## Adaptation Rule

Do not reuse the same visual and narrative template across all deck types.

At minimum, adapt:

- Title style
- Density
- Amount of data per slide
- Role of speaker notes
- Whether the audience needs persuasion, instruction, or evidence
